## Element Scoring Details

### 1. Character (Hero = Customer) — Weight: 1.2x

| Score | What It Looks Like |
|-------|-------------------|
| 1-2 | Homepage/content is about the company. No customer desire. "Founded in 2003, we are an award-winning..." |
| 3-4 | Customer vaguely implied. Desire unclear. "We help businesses succeed." |
| 5-6 | Customer named, desire is soft/broad. "Small business owners who want to grow." |
| 7-8 | Specific customer, specific desire. "Busy parents who want healthy dinners in 20 minutes." |
| 9-10 | Vivid, urgent. Reader immediately self-identifies. Aspirational identity invoked. |

**Red flags:** "We" appears before customer. Origin story leads. No identifiable human addressed.
**Green flags:** Opens with "you." Desire is specific + emotionally resonant. Reader self-selects in 5 seconds.

### 2. Problem — Weight: 1.3x

| Score | What It Looks Like |
|-------|-------------------|
| 1-2 | No problem. All features. "We offer cloud-based software with 50+ integrations." |
| 3-4 | External problem only, vague. "Managing projects is hard." |
| 5-6 | External clear, internal hinted. No philosophical layer. |
| 7-8 | External + Internal clearly stated. |
| 9-10 | All three layers + clear villain. Customer feels deeply understood. |

**Red flags:** Reads like a spec sheet. No emotional tension. Can't complete "It's just wrong that..."
**Green flags:** Villain named. Internal frustration uses emotional language. Philosophical statement resonates.

### 3. Guide — Weight: 1.0x

| Score | What It Looks Like |
|-------|-------------------|
| 1-2 | Brand is hero. "We're the #1 provider." Zero empathy. |
| 3-4 | Authority present, no empathy. Credentials listed, pain unacknowledged. |
| 5-6 | Empathy present, authority weak. "We get it" but no proof of competence. |
| 7-8 | Both present. Empathetic + proof (testimonials, stats, logos). |
| 9-10 | Seamless guide positioning. Empathy feels genuine. Authority demonstrated, not claimed. |

**Red flags:** "We" count >> "you" count. Company journey dominates. No social proof.
**Green flags:** Empathy specific to internal problem. Authority via results. Brand feels like trusted advisor.

### 4. Plan — Weight: 1.0x

| Score | What It Looks Like |
|-------|-------------------|
| 1-2 | No plan. Customer must figure out how to engage. |
| 3-4 | Plan exists but buried, complex (6+ steps), or uses jargon. |
| 5-6 | 3-4 steps but uses internal language. Reader has to think. |
| 7-8 | Clear 3-step plan, customer-centric language. |
| 9-10 | Elegant 3 steps + agreement plan (guarantee/risk reduction). "This is easy and safe." |

**Red flags:** No "how it works." Customers ask "So how does this actually work?" No risk reduction.
**Green flags:** Exactly 3 steps. Simple active verbs. Implicit promise of ease.

### 5. Call to Action — Weight: 1.3x

| Score | What It Looks Like |
|-------|-------------------|
| 1-2 | No CTA. Only option is "Contact Us" hidden in nav. |
| 3-4 | CTA exists but weak/hidden. "Learn More" in small text. |
| 5-6 | Direct CTA present, reasonably visible. Appears once. No transitional CTA. |
| 7-8 | Strong direct CTA + transitional CTA. Both visible. |
| 9-10 | Direct CTA bold, specific, above fold, repeated 3-5x. Transitional CTA captures non-ready leads. |

**Red flags:** Zero CTAs. "Submit" on contact form. CTA only in footer.
**Green flags:** CTA text describes outcome ("Get My Free Plan" > "Submit"). Repeated. Transitional CTA valuable enough to collect leads.

### 6. Failure (Stakes) — Weight: 0.8x

| Score | What It Looks Like |
|-------|-------------------|
| 1-2 | Zero stakes. Everything sunny. No urgency. |
| 3-4 | Generic stakes. "Don't miss out!" |
| 5-6 | Stakes named but feel distant/abstract. |
| 7-8 | Specific, real stakes. "Your competitors are getting the calls you should be getting." |
| 9-10 | Vivid, proportionate urgency. Connects to internal problem. Motivates without alienating. |

**Red flags:** No consequence statements anywhere. No urgency to act now vs never.
**Green flags:** Failure connects to internal/philosophical problem. Tone is concerned guide, not fearmonger.

### 7. Success — Weight: 1.0x

| Score | What It Looks Like |
|-------|-------------------|
| 1-2 | No success vision. Ends with product descriptions. |
| 3-4 | Generic. "Grow your business." "Achieve more." |
| 5-6 | Somewhat specific. "Save 10 hours a week." But no emotional transformation. |
| 7-8 | Specific + emotional. Functional outcome + how it feels. |
| 9-10 | Complete transformation — status, emotion, identity. Before/after contrast vivid. Reader can picture themselves. |

**Red flags:** No outcomes mentioned. Success is about the company. Generic buzzwords.
**Green flags:** Before/after contrast. Addresses all three problem layers. Specific + emotional.

---

## Composite Scoring Formula

```
Weighted Score = (Character × 1.2) + (Problem × 1.3) + (Guide × 1.0)
              + (Plan × 1.0) + (CTA × 1.3) + (Failure × 0.8) + (Success × 1.0)

Final Score = Weighted Score − Penalties + Grunt Test Modifier
Max = 86
```

**Grunt Test Modifier:** 3/3 = +5 | 2/3 = 0 | 1/3 = −5 | 0/3 = −10

## Bonus Scoring (Website Homepages Only)

### Hero Image (+0 to +3)
StoryBrand emphasizes that the above-fold image should show the customer in their SUCCESS state — not the company's building, team photo, or product in isolation.

| Score | What It Looks Like |
|-------|-------------------|
| +0 | No image, irrelevant stock photo, or company-centric image (team photo, office building, logo) |
| +1 | Relevant image but generic (stock photo of person at computer, abstract visualization) |
| +2 | Product screenshot or data visualization that implies the outcome |
| +3 | Aspirational image showing the customer in the success state, or powerful visual proof of the product's value (e.g., before/after satellite imagery for a satellite product) |

### Hero Specificity (+0 to +2)
StoryBrand demands ONE hero with ONE desire. Multiple competing audiences above the fold dilutes the story.

| Score | What It Looks Like |
|-------|-------------------|
| +0 | 4+ distinct audience personas given equal weight; reader can't self-select quickly |
| +1 | Multiple audiences but one is clearly primary above the fold; others are below the fold |
| +2 | One specific hero named or strongly implied in the headline/sub-headline; reader self-selects within 3 seconds |

**Updated Max Score: 86 (base) + 5 (bonuses) = 91**

## Deduction Patterns

| Pattern | Minor (−1) | Moderate (−3) | Severe (−5) |
|---------|-----------|--------------|-------------|
| **Jargon** | 1-2 replaceable terms | 3-5 terms or unexplained branded methodology | Glossary needed |
| **Company-as-Hero** | "We" before "you" in opening | >25% is company story | Entire piece about company |
| **Missing CTA** | Weak language ("Learn More") | Buried (bottom only) | None at all |
| **Confusion** | One competing message | Multiple value props fight | Creates more questions than answers |
| **No Stakes** | Vague ("Don't miss out") | Zero mention (−2 only) | — |

**Quick test:** Count "we/us/our" vs "you/your." If we-count > you-count → at least minor Company-as-Hero penalty.

## Grading Scale

| Score | Grade | Action |
|-------|-------|--------|
| 80-91 | **A** | Exceptional. Minor tweaks only. Ship it. |
| 65-79 | **B** | Strong foundation. 2-3 targeted improvements will elevate it. |
| 50-64 | **C** | Functional but missing key elements. Significant revision needed. |
| 35-49 | **D** | Multiple structural problems. Major rewrite recommended. |
| <35 | **F** | Fundamentally broken messaging. Start over from BrandScript. |

---

## Fix Priority Order

1. Grunt Test failures (clarity first)
2. Character/Hero positioning (who's the hero?)
3. Problem articulation (the hook)
4. Call to Action (no CTA = no conversion)
5. Plan (reduce friction)
6. Guide positioning (empathy + authority)
7. Stakes/Failure (urgency)
8. Success/Transformation (motivation)

## Quick Wins (typically <1 hour)

| Fix | Impact | Effort |
|-----|--------|--------|
| Rewrite headline to name customer's problem/desire | Very High | 15 min |
| Change CTA button text to outcome-based language | High | 5 min |
| Add 3-step plan section | High | 30 min |
| Move first CTA above the fold | High | 10 min |
| Replace "We are..." opening with "You..." | High | 10 min |
| Add one sentence naming stakes of inaction | Medium | 10 min |
| Replace 3-5 jargon terms with plain language | Medium | 20 min |

## Rewrite Engine — Element-by-Element Fix Patterns

When an element scores ≤7, generate a specific rewrite. Use these patterns per element.

### Fix Pattern: Character (Hero)

**Diagnosis:** If the content opens with "We" or the company name, or the customer's desire is vague/missing.

**Fix template:**
```
BEFORE: "We are the leading provider of [product category]."
AFTER:  "[Specific desire statement from customer's perspective]."
```

**Rewrite rules:**
- Replace every "We are/We offer/We provide" opening with "You want/You need/You deserve"
- Define ONE specific desire. Use the "survival test" — does it connect to: saving money, saving time, gaining status, building connections, finding meaning, or reducing risk?
- The desire should be completable: "I want _____ so I can _____."
- If multiple audiences exist, pick the ONE that represents >50% of revenue and write for them. Name others below the fold only.

**Rewrite examples by industry:**
| Industry | Weak | Strong |
|----------|------|--------|
| SaaS | "We provide cloud-based project management" | "Ship projects on time without the chaos" |
| Financial advisor | "Comprehensive wealth management services" | "A retirement plan that lets you stop worrying" |
| Cybersecurity | "Enterprise-grade security solutions" | "Stop wondering if your data is safe" |
| Marketing agency | "We're a full-service digital agency" | "Get more customers from your website — without guessing what works" |

---

### Fix Pattern: Problem

**Diagnosis:** If the content only states the external problem, or jumps straight to features with no problem at all.

**Fix template — always write all three layers:**
```
EXTERNAL: [The tangible obstacle — what they'd Google]
INTERNAL: [How it makes them FEEL — frustration, embarrassment, anxiety, incompetence]
PHILOSOPHICAL: [Why it's just WRONG — "No one should have to..." / "It's not right that..."]
VILLAIN: [The root cause — a force, not a person. Complexity, outdated systems, opacity, etc.]
```

**Rewrite rules:**
- The external problem is necessary but NOT sufficient. It's the setup.
- The internal problem is where purchases happen. Name the emotion explicitly: frustrated, overwhelmed, embarrassed, anxious, behind, incompetent, stuck, exhausted, exposed.
- The philosophical problem elevates the transaction to a cause. Use "should/shouldn't" language.
- Name ONE villain. Make it a force the reader already resents.

**Escalation technique — build tension across sentences:**
```
WEAK:  "Managing your finances is complicated."
BUILT: "You've spent years building this business. But every time tax season
        hits, you're buried in spreadsheets, terrified you're missing something
        that could cost you everything. You shouldn't need an accounting degree
        to keep what you've earned."
```
This hits external (complicated finances) → internal (terrified, buried) → philosophical (shouldn't need a degree).

### Fix Pattern: Guide

**Diagnosis:** If the brand is positioned as hero (bragging, origin story, awards list) or has empathy without authority, or authority without empathy.

**Fix template — always include BOTH:**
```
EMPATHY: "We understand [specific internal problem]. We've [seen it / lived it / heard it from hundreds of clients]."
AUTHORITY: [Pick 2-3 from: testimonial quote, client count, specific result stat, recognizable logo, relevant credential]
```

**Rewrite rules:**
- Empathy MUST reference the internal problem from Element 2. Generic empathy ("We care about our clients") is worthless.
- Authority should be demonstrated, not claimed. "Trusted by 500 companies" > "We're the industry leader."
- The ideal ratio: 1 empathy statement → 2-3 authority signals.
- Testimonials are the strongest authority signal. A specific client result ("increased revenue 40% in 6 months") beats a vague endorsement ("Great company to work with!").
- If no real testimonials exist, flag this as the #1 recommendation and suggest: client count, years in business, specific results delivered, industry credentials, or media mentions.

**Empathy rewrite examples:**
| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| "We care about your success" | "We know what it's like to stare at a forecast and wonder if half the projects in it are real" |
| "Customer-first approach" | "You shouldn't have to gamble millions on data that's already months old" |
| "We're passionate about helping businesses grow" | "We've watched smart companies lose deals because their data was stale. It's why we built this." |

**Authority rewrite examples:**
| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| "Award-winning platform" | "Trusted by 12 of the top 20 power trading desks" |
| "Industry leader since 2015" | "Our clients identified 47 construction delays before the market last year" |
| "We've helped thousands of customers" | "'Vulcan paid for itself in a single trade.' — Senior Analyst, Fortune 500 energy firm" |

### Fix Pattern: Plan

**Diagnosis:** If there's no clear process, or the process has >4 steps, or uses jargon.

**Fix template:**
```
PROCESS PLAN (required):
  Step 1: [Simple verb] — [One sentence, plain language]
  Step 2: [Simple verb] — [One sentence]
  Step 3: [Simple verb] — [One sentence, ends with the outcome]

AGREEMENT PLAN (recommended):
  "[Promise that reduces the customer's biggest fear about buying]"
```

**Rewrite rules:**
- ALWAYS use exactly 3 steps. If the real process has 8 steps, abstract to 3 phases.
- Step 1 = the LOWEST friction action (schedule, sign up, download — not "commit" or "purchase")
- Step 3 = should NAME the success outcome, not just the process. "Watch your pipeline grow" not "Receive onboarding."
- Name each step with a simple verb: Schedule, Get, See, Start, Build, Launch, Discover.
- The agreement plan addresses the #1 buying fear. Common ones: "No long-term contracts," "Cancel anytime," "Your data stays yours," "Money-back guarantee," "No technical setup required."

**Plan rewrite examples:**
| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| "Contact us → We'll assess your needs → Implementation → Training → Go-live → Ongoing support" | "1. Schedule a call. 2. Get your custom plan. 3. See results in 30 days." |
| "Submit your information via our online portal" | "1. Book a free 15-minute demo. 2. We build your dashboard. 3. Start making decisions with confidence." |

### Fix Pattern: Call to Action

**Diagnosis:** If CTA is missing, weak ("Learn More," "Submit"), hidden, or appears only once.

**Fix template:**
```
DIRECT CTA: "[Action verb] + [Specific outcome]"
  → Must appear: above fold, mid-page, end of page (minimum 3x)
  → Must be: bold button, contrasting color, same wording every time

TRANSITIONAL CTA: "[Get/Download/Watch] + [Free valuable resource]"
  → Must appear: near the direct CTA (secondary visual weight)
  → Must be: genuinely valuable enough to justify giving an email
```

**Rewrite rules:**
- CTA button text should describe what the customer GETS, not what they DO.
  - "Submit" → "Get My Free Plan"
  - "Learn More" → "See How It Works"
  - "Contact Us" → "Schedule Your Free Strategy Call"
  - "Sign Up" → "Start Your Free Trial"
- Direct CTA wording must be IDENTICAL everywhere it appears. Don't rotate between "Schedule a Demo" / "Get in Touch" / "Talk to Us" — pick one.
- Transitional CTA must offer real value: checklist, guide, assessment, webinar, sample report. Not "Subscribe to our newsletter" (low perceived value).
- Visual hierarchy: Direct CTA = filled button, bright/contrasting color. Transitional CTA = outlined button or text link.

**CTA strength ladder (weakest → strongest):**
1. ❌ "Submit" / "Click Here" / "Learn More" (zero value language)
2. 😐 "Contact Us" / "Get in Touch" (vague, puts burden on customer)
3. ✅ "Schedule a Call" / "Start Free Trial" (clear action)
4. 🔥 "Get Your Free Growth Plan" / "See Your Score" / "Start Saving Today" (outcome-focused)

### Fix Pattern: Failure (Stakes)

**Diagnosis:** If there's no mention of consequences, or stakes are generic ("Don't miss out").

**Fix template:**
```
STAKES STATEMENT: "Without [solution], [specific negative consequence that connects to the internal problem]."
```

**Rewrite rules:**
- Stakes must be SPECIFIC. Replace "you'll fall behind" with a scenario the reader can visualize.
- Connect stakes to the INTERNAL problem. Not just "you'll lose money" but "you'll keep lying awake wondering if your forecast is wrong."
- Use competitor framing when appropriate: "While you're working with stale data, your competitors who have this information are already repositioning."
- The tone should be a concerned guide, NOT a fearmonger. Imagine a trusted advisor saying it over coffee, not a used car salesman creating panic.
- Proportionality matters: stakes should match the actual severity. Overstating destroys credibility.

**Stakes rewrite examples:**
| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| "Don't miss out on this opportunity" | "Every month without clear messaging, you're spending money on marketing that quietly fails — and you won't know why until the budget's gone" |
| "Act now before it's too late" | "Your competitor with the clearer website is getting the phone calls that should be going to you. Right now. Today." |
| "You need this" | "The last firm that ignored construction delays in their generation model mispriced a forward contract by eight figures. They found out from an EIA filing — four months after the market had already moved." |

**Calibration guide:**
- B2C / emotional purchases: can lean slightly more into fear (safety, family, health)
- B2B / rational purchases: lean into competitive disadvantage and financial cost of inaction
- Both: always end stakes with an implicit "but there's a better path" — the CTA should follow immediately

---

### Fix Pattern: Success (Transformation)

**Diagnosis:** If outcomes are generic ("grow your business"), feature-focused ("get access to our dashboard"), or missing entirely.

**Fix template:**
```
FUNCTIONAL OUTCOME: [What changes in their external world — specific, measurable]
EMOTIONAL OUTCOME: [How they FEEL — specific emotion named]
IDENTITY SHIFT: [Who they BECOME — the character transformation]

FULL TRANSFORMATION: "You go from [before state] to [after state]."
```

**Rewrite rules:**
- NEVER stop at functional outcomes. "Save 10 hours/week" is necessary but insufficient.
- Always add the emotional layer: "Save 10 hours/week — and stop dreading Monday mornings."
- The identity shift is the crown jewel. It answers: "Who do I become?"
  - From overwhelmed → in control
  - From behind → ahead of the curve
  - From guessing → knowing
  - From reactive → proactive
  - From anxious → confident
  - From invisible → respected
- Use "imagine" or "picture this" language to help the reader visualize.
- Pair with a testimonial that shows the before/after if possible.

**Transformation rewrite examples:**
| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| "Grow your business" | "Walk into your Monday meeting knowing your pipeline is full, your forecast is accurate, and your team knows exactly what to do. You're not the overwhelmed operator anymore — you're the CEO." |
| "Access powerful analytics" | "Stop squinting at spreadsheets hoping the numbers are right. See your entire business in one dashboard and make decisions in minutes instead of days. For the first time, the data works for you — not the other way around." |
| "Get more leads" | "Imagine checking your inbox and seeing qualified prospects reaching out to YOU — because your website finally tells the story your business deserves. No more wondering if anyone's listening." |

## Before/After Rewrite Output Format

For each element scored ≤7, generate a rewrite using this exact format:

```markdown
### [Element Name] — Score: X/10

**CURRENT:**
> [Exact quote from the content. If element is absent, write "(Not present — no [element] content found)"]

**DIAGNOSIS:**
[One sentence: what's wrong and which StoryBrand principle it violates]

**RECOMMENDED REWRITE:**
> [Complete rewritten version that would score 8+. Not a vague suggestion — actual copy they can paste in.]

**WHY THIS WORKS:**
[One sentence connecting the fix to StoryBrand psychology]

**EFFORT:** [5 min / 15 min / 30 min / 1 hour / requires external input]
```

**Critical rules for rewrites:**
- ALWAYS provide actual copy, not directions. "Make the headline more customer-focused" is useless. "Your retirement is too important for guesswork" is actionable.
- Match the brand's existing tone and vocabulary. Don't rewrite a casual brand in corporate language or vice versa.
- Keep word counts similar to the original unless brevity is the fix.
- If the fix requires information you don't have (real testimonials, specific metrics, client logos), write the best version possible AND flag what's needed. Example: "REQUIRES: One real client quote with a specific result. Placeholder shown below — replace the bracketed sections."
- For websites, specify WHERE the rewrite goes (above fold, after the plan section, etc.)

## Structural Recommendations Format

After element-by-element rewrites, provide structural recommendations for any systemic issues. Use this format:

```markdown
## Structural Recommendations

### Priority 1: [Name] — [Impact: High/Medium] | [Effort: Low/Medium/High]
**What:** [One sentence description]
**Why:** [One sentence StoryBrand reasoning]
**How:** [Specific, actionable steps — not vague directions]
**Requires:** [Any external input needed — client quotes, photos, data, etc.]

### Priority 2: ...
```

**Common structural recommendations to consider:**
1. **Full BrandScript needed** — when no clear story arc exists at all
2. **Audience consolidation** — when multiple personas compete with no primary
3. **Section reordering** — when the page doesn't follow the SB7 narrative flow
4. **Visual proof needed** — when the product claims are strong but unsubstantiated visually
5. **Transitional CTA creation** — when there's a hard sell but no soft entry for non-ready visitors
6. **Testimonial/case study collection** — when authority relies entirely on self-sourced claims
7. **Page length reduction** — when the page is so long it dilutes the story
8. **Mobile audit** — when the desktop structure may break the Grunt Test on mobile (CTA below fold, headline wrapping, etc.)

## Content-Type Quick Checks

### Website Homepage (10 criteria)
Above-fold Grunt Test pass, aspirational hero image, direct CTA above fold, transitional CTA present, stakes section, 3-step plan, empathy + authority, success/transformation, minimal nav (≤7), one clear storyline

### Email
Subject line opens story loop, first line about reader, one problem named, guide positioning, single CTA, stakes/urgency, success described, brevity (<200 words for sales)

### Sales Deck
Opens with customer's problem (not logo), 3-layer problem covered, guide empathy + authority, 3-step plan, stakes slide, success + proof, specific singular CTA, ≤15 slides, customer-centric language throughout

### Social Media
Hook names problem/desire, customer is hero, value delivered, one CTA, scannable format, stakes or success mentioned

### Video Script
Opens with problem (first 10 sec), viewer is hero, problem escalated, guide with empathy + authority, simple plan, success shown, specific CTA spoken, under 2 minutes
